Fires

Firefighters gird for potential flare-up of the Deception Complex fire

 
 
 
 
 
 

By Christian Hill

The Register-Guard

9:58 a.m., Aug. 28, 2014

5

OAKRIDGE — Crews are girding for another flare-up of the Deception Complex fire this afternoon as about 100 residents outside Oakridge are on alert for a possible evacuation.

Officials briefly evacuated a mobile home park along Highway 58 and about 60 homes along LaDuke Road on Wednesday evening after weather conditions allowed the fire to more than double in size to 1,832 acres from 851 acres. The residents remain on a Level II — or be prepared to leave — evacuation alert. The Middle Fork ranger station is closed.

Wednesday’s flareup threw up a huge plume of smoke, visible in the Eugene-Springfield area, and caused air quality in Oakridge to deteriorate to unhealthy levels before improving overnight.

Around noon today, the Lane Regional Air Protection Authority reported air quality again deteriorated to unhealthy levels due to smoke in Oakridge and surrounding areas. LRAPA advised residents to stay indoors, if possible, and limit outdoor activity, especially for children, senior citizens and people with heart and lung conditions.

The smoke has drifted into Bend and Cottage Grove, and the Waldo Lake Recreation Area further east in the Willamette National Forest was reported to be very smoky.

Rick Acosta, spokesman for the interagency team leading the firefighting efforts, said crews could see increased fire activity again starting after 2 p.m. as the temperature rises and weather conditions provide more oxygen to the wildfire.

Cooler temperatures are expected Friday.

“This may be the last day of hot weather that provides energy to the fire,” he said.

More than 1,000 personnel are on scene fighting the fire, which has left the eastern Lane County town shrouded in a smoky haze. Helicopters dumping water and retardant on the fire are aiding crews on the ground digging fire lines.

The fire is centered west of Highway 58 over a tall forested ridgeline outside of Oakridge.

Lightning storms caused the first fires in July, with an Aug. 10 storm causing at least one new fire in the area.

The fire was about half contained Wednesday, but weather conditions allowed three smaller fires to merge and then enabled the blaze to jump containment lines and run up the ridgeline separating it from the homes, officials reported.

Burning debris ignited a few small spot fires on the hillside above the homes before firefighters quickly extinguished them, they said. No structures were lost.

Residents on alert this morning for possible evacuation took the news in stride.

Jerry Shortt, 70, who live on LaDuke Road, said he and his cousin spent hours Wednesday watering down his seven-acre property with sprinklers and hoses. It was the closest wildfire has gotten in the nearly 40 years he’s lived on the property, Short said. The fire was kept at bay Wednesday night, but burnt leaves rained down on his property.

The two men were again wetting down the property this morning, and their wives had packed up their motorhomes in case they got the evacuation order.

“We’ll have to do what we have to do,” he said. “There’s nothing else we can do about it.”

At the Deception Creek Mobile Home Park, Phil Macabee, 76, was also gathering up belongings to return to his daughter’s home in Oakridge.

The fire has been burning for more than two weeks, with water-laden helicopters over the mobile home park during that time. The smoke has been keeping away from the area, making it more comfortable for the residents, he said, but everything changed when the wind shifted Wednesday.

Macabee was attending a informational meeting Wednesday afternoon at the ranger station when he was told to go home due to the Level II alert.

He returned home to find the plume of smoke rising near the mobile home park, turning the setting sun red.

A neighbor told him about the evacuation order around 6 p.m., Macabee said, and he, his wife and their seven cats left to stay the night at their daughter’s home.

Macabee said he wasn’t getting worked up about the uncertainty because the fire was out of his control.

“You can’t accomplish anything worrying,” he said. “You have to have a plan of action and act on it.”

He also praised the work of the firefighters.

“They’ve done as good a job as humanly possible,” he said.
